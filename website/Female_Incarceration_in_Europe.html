Chapter II · Framework

Women Behind Bars: Gender, Society, and the Making of the Female Criminal

For most of recorded history, the story of crime has been told from a male perspective. Courtrooms, prisons and criminological textbooks have long centred on the concept of the deviant or dangerous man who breaks the rules. When women appeared in this narrative at all, they were treated as footnotes: anomalies, exceptions and curiosities. However, precisely because female criminality was considered so unusual, it has always held a disproportionate symbolic significance. When a woman transgressed, she did not merely break the law. She broke an entire social order.

To understand why, we must go back to the beginning.

A Body Destined for Virtue

The earliest scientific attempts to explain female crime were not really about crime at all. They were about gender. In his 1895 work La donna delinquente, la prostituta e la donna normale, Cesare Lombroso argued that female criminals were evolutionary throwbacks, atavistic creatures who combined the worst feminine traits (deceit, cunning) with unwomanly masculine characteristics such as aggression and cold rationality. The "normal" woman, by contrast, was passive, maternal, and morally superior precisely because of her physical and intellectual limitations.

This framework was not neutral science. It was ideology disguised as measurement. A criminal woman was considered dangerous not only because she had broken the law, but also because she had abandoned her assigned role. According to Lombroso, she was doubly deviant: deviant against society and deviant against her own nature.

William Thomas (The Unadjusted Girl, 1923) softened the biological edge but preserved the moral architecture: women committed crimes when they failed to satisfy their innate need for love and belonging through legitimate channels, primarily marriage and motherhood. Otto Pollak (The Criminality of Women, 1950) argued that women's crimes were often less visible, both because women were supposedly better at concealing them and because the justice system tended to treat them more leniently than men.

Does Equality Change the Picture?

This reframing sparked a debate that continues to this day. In 1975, Freda Adler of Sisters in Crime proposed the emancipation thesis, which posited that as women gained access to education, employment and public life, their crime rates would converge with those of men. This idea was intuitive and alarming to those who viewed women's liberation as a social threat.

"Why are women so effectively kept from crime?"

It took until 1968 for a genuinely new vocabulary to emerge. Frances Heidensohn's landmark article The Deviance of Women: A Critique and an Inquiry argued that the low rates of female crime had nothing to do with biological virtue or moral superiority. They were the result of control, of the relentless surveillance exercised over women's bodies, movements, and reputations from childhood onwards. Women committed fewer crimes because they had fewer opportunities to commit them, and far more to lose if they did.

Increased female participation in crime, where it existed, was a symptom not of empowerment but of continued disadvantage.

However, the data told a more complicated story. Meda Chesney-Lind's marginalisation theory provided the most convincing alternative narrative: the women entering the criminal justice system were not the liberated individuals that Adler had imagined. They were the most marginalized, driven to petty theft, drug offences, and survival crimes by poverty, precarity, and a labour market that had never been equitable in the first place.

The Central Question

Which perspective does the European evidence support? Using ten years of prison data across 27 EU member states (2014–2023), this project asks a deceptively simple question: do countries where women and men are more equal also imprison fewer women? The answer, as the data will show, is more nuanced and more theoretically interesting than either a straightforward yes or no.

Chapter III · Methods

How We Measured It

This project integrates two publicly available datasets covering 27 European Union member states over the period 2014–2023.

Source 01 · Eurostat

Prison Data

Prison population data were sourced from Eurostat, which compiles annual statistics on persons held in penal institutions across EU member states, disaggregated by sex, age class, and country. From this dataset, two core variables were derived: the number of female prisoners per 100,000 inhabitants (PerHundred), computed using annual national population figures, and the female share of the total prison population (% Female), calculated as the ratio of female to total prisoners for each country-year observation. Both metrics use the Age class Total filter. The EU aggregate was excluded from country-level analysis and treated separately as a reference figure.

Source 02 · WEF Gender Gap Report

Gender Equality Data

Gender equality data were drawn from the World Economic Forum's Global Gender Gap Report, published annually since 2006. The WEF Gender Gap Index scores countries on a 0-to-1 scale across four dimensions: economic participation and opportunity, educational attainment, health and survival, and political empowerment, where 1 represents full parity. Annual scores for each EU member state were merged with the Eurostat prison data by country and year, yielding a panel dataset of 270 country-year observations with no missing values on either key variable.

Method · Python · Tableau · Power BI

Analysis & Tools

The datasets were cleaned, integrated, and explored using Python (Pandas, NumPy and Plotly). All 27 EU member states were retained. The core statistical analysis consists of bivariate correlation tested using both Pearson's r and Spearman's ρ, allowing us to assess whether any association is linear or driven by specific country clusters. No causal claims are made: the analysis is explicitly exploratory.

Limitation: data comparability across European countries may be affected by differences in how national institutions collect, classify, and report prison statistics. The lack of full methodological uniformity means that cross-country comparisons should be interpreted with caution.

Chapter IX · What the Evidence Shows

Key Findings & What the Data Tell Us

What the Data Shows: Europe, 2014–2023

Across 27 EU member states over the period 2014–2023, women remain a small and remarkably stable minority within prison populations.

5.70% of the total prison population

On average, women represent just *5.66%* of the total prison population, a figure that has barely shifted over the decade, moving from 5.34% in 2014 to 5.79% in 2023 (Δ = +0.45 percentage points). The rate of female imprisonment per 100,000 inhabitants has been equally static: 6.84 in 2014 and 6.81 in 2023 (Δ = −0.03).

However, significant variation exists across countries beneath this overall stability. Latvia, the Czech Republic, Hungary, Slovakia and Malta have the highest rates of female imprisonment per 100,000 inhabitants, ranging from around 10.5 to 15.2. All five of these countries have Gender Gap Index averages between 0.68 and 0.70, which are among the lowest in the EU. At the other end of the spectrum, Denmark, Ireland, Bulgaria, Cyprus and the Netherlands imprison fewer than 3.5 women per 100,000 inhabitants on average.

The female share of total prison population follows a partially different geography, and here the pattern is more complex. Malta (8.13%), Latvia (7.97%), Czechia (7.72%), Finland (7.60%), and Hungary (7.53%) show the highest proportions, while France (3.34%), Bulgaria (3.39%), Ireland (3.93%), and Italy and Poland (both 4.27%) show the lowest. Finland's presence in the top five for female share is a telling anomaly: despite recording just 4.10 female prisoners per 100,000 and one of the highest gender equality scores in Europe (0.84), its high female share reflects not elevated female incarceration, but exceptionally low male incarceration, which mechanically inflates the female proportion.

Key Findings: Correlations

Testing both measures of female incarceration against the Gender Gap Index across all 270 country-year observations yields two distinct results.

Pearson r = −0.330 · Spearman ρ = −0.362 · p < 0.001

Female prisoners per 100,000 inhabitants shows a statistically significant negative correlation with the Gender Gap Index (Pearson r = −0.360, Spearman r = −0.377, p < 0.001 for both). The agreement between the two coefficients, one assuming linearity and one entirely rank-based, strengthens the finding considerably. Countries with higher gender equality consistently imprison fewer women relative to their population, and this relationship is not an artefact of outliers or distributional skew.

r = −0.045, p = 0.457

Female share of total prisoners shows no meaningful association. Both Pearson (r = −0.078, p = 0.203) and Spearman (ρ = −0.112, p = 0.067) correlations fall well below conventional thresholds of statistical significance. Gender equality has no detectable relationship with how women are represented within prison populations relative to men.

This divergence between the two metrics is itself a substantive finding. When societies become more gender-equal, the absolute rate at which women are imprisoned tends to decrease, but this reduction appears to occur in parallel with comparable shifts in male incarceration, leaving the internal gender ratio of prisons largely unchanged. The driver appears to be a systemic effect on criminality and criminal justice broadly, rather than a gender-specific one.

What Theories Tell Us — After the Data

These findings address the long-standing theoretical debate directly. The emancipation thesis predicted that greater gender equality would lead to higher female crime rates, but this idea is not supported by the European data. In fact, the relationship appears to run in the opposite direction. This aligns with Chesney-Lind's marginalisation theory, which states that structural disadvantage, rather than empowerment, is what drives women into contact with criminal justice systems. Where gender equality genuinely improves, fewer women end up in prison.

The finding also resonates with Nordic research showing that the closing gender gap in crime over recent decades is driven primarily by a decline in male offending rather than a rise in female criminality (Estrada et al., British Journal of Criminology, 2016). In this reading, equality does not bring women's crime rates up to men's. Rather, it brings men closer to women's historically lower rates.

What the data cannot resolve is the direction of causality. Countries with high gender equality also differ in ways that independently shape criminal justice outcomes: welfare state generosity, incarceration philosophy, policing practices, and legal culture. Disentangling these factors would require longitudinal panel regression beyond the scope of this project. What the data does clearly challenge is a deeply held assumption: that more equal societies will see women's behaviour, including criminal behaviour, converge with men's. The European evidence, at least, suggests the opposite.
