Chapter II · Framework

Women Behind Bars: Gender, Society, and the Making of the Female Criminal

For most of recorded history, the story of crime has been told as a man's story. Courtrooms, prisons, and criminological textbooks have long been built around a masculine subject. When women appeared at all, they were footnotes — anomalies, exceptions, curiosities. Yet, precisely because female criminality was considered so rare, it has always carried a disproportionate symbolic weight.

§ 01

A Body Destined for Virtue

The earliest scientific attempts to explain female crime were not really about crime at all. They were about gender. In La donna delinquente (1895), Cesare Lombroso argued that female criminals were evolutionary throwbacks — atavistic creatures combining the worst traits of femininity with unwomanly masculine characteristics. The "normal" woman, by contrast, was passive, maternal, morally superior precisely because of her physical and intellectual limitations.

This framework was not neutral science. It was ideology dressed in measurement. The criminal woman was dangerous not just because she had broken a law, but because she had abandoned her assigned role — doubly deviant, against society and against her own nature.

William Thomas (1923) softened the biological edge but preserved the moral architecture. Otto Pollak (1950) added that women's crimes were simply hidden — concealed by deception and protected by a chivalrous justice system.

"Why are women so effectively kept from crime?"

It took until 1968 for a genuinely new vocabulary. Frances Heidensohn argued that low rates of female crime had nothing to do with biological virtue — they were the result of control. Women committed fewer crimes because they had fewer opportunities, and far more to lose if they did.

§ 02

Does Equality Change the Picture?

This reframing opened a debate that continues to this day. In 1975, Freda Adler proposed the emancipation thesis: as women gained access to education, employment, and public life, their crime rates would converge with men's. The argument was intuitive — and immediately alarming to those who saw women's liberation as a social threat.

Increased female participation in crime, where it existed, was a symptom not of empowerment but of continued disadvantage.

The data, however, told a more complicated story. Meda Chesney-Lind's marginalization theory offered the most compelling counter-narrative: women in the criminal justice system were not Adler's liberated women asserting newfound power. They were the most marginalized — driven to petty theft, drug offences, and survival crimes by poverty and a labour market that had never been equitable in the first place.

Subsequent research has largely validated this view. Studies on Nordic countries — among the most gender-equal in the world — show that the closing gender gap in crime is driven primarily by a decline in male offending, not a rise in female criminality (Estrada et al., BJC, 2016).

§ 03

The Central Question

Which perspective does the European evidence support? Using ten years of prison data across 27 EU member states (2014–2023), this project asks a deceptively simple question: do countries where women and men are more equal also imprison fewer women? The answer, as the data will show, is more nuanced — and more theoretically interesting — than either a straightforward yes or no.

Chapter IX · What the Evidence Shows

Key Findings & What the Data Tell Us

What the Data Shows: Europe, 2014–2023

Across all 27 EU member states over the decade, women remain a small and remarkably stable minority within prison populations.

5.70% of the total prison population

On average, women represent 5.70% of prison populations — a figure that has barely shifted, moving from 5.34% in 2014 to 5.79% in 2023 (Δ = +0.45 percentage points). The rate of female imprisonment per 100,000 inhabitants has been equally static: 6.84 in 2014, 6.81 in 2023 (Δ = −0.03). Whatever is happening across European societies over this decade, it is not producing any meaningful change in the scale of female incarceration.

Beneath this overall stability, however, significant cross-country variation exists. The countries with the highest rates of female imprisonment per 100,000 inhabitants are Latvia (15.18), Czechia (14.75), Hungary (13.63), Slovakia (13.06), and Malta (10.52) — all Eastern or Southern European countries with Gender Gap Index scores ranging from 0.68 to 0.70. At the other end, Denmark (2.84), Bulgaria (3.31), Cyprus (3.36), the Netherlands (3.39), and France (3.42) imprison the fewest women per 100,000 inhabitants.

The female share of prison populations tells a partially different story. Malta (8.13%), Latvia (7.97%), Czechia (7.72%), Finland (7.60%), and Hungary (7.53%) show the highest proportions. Finland is a notable anomaly: the highest female share alongside one of the lowest absolute rates per 100,000 — a reminder that the two metrics measure fundamentally different things.

Key Findings: Correlations

Testing both measures against the Gender Gap Index across all 270 country-year observations yields two sharply divergent results.

Pearson r = −0.330 · Spearman ρ = −0.362 · p < 0.001

Female prisoners per 100,000 inhabitants shows a statistically significant negative correlation with the Gender Gap Index. The alignment between the parametric and non-parametric coefficients confirms that the relationship is not an artefact of distributional assumptions or driven by outliers: countries with higher gender equality consistently imprison fewer women relative to their population across the entire decade.

r = −0.045, p = 0.457

Female share of total prisoners shows no meaningful association. Both Pearson (r = −0.045, p = 0.457) and Spearman (ρ = −0.097, p = 0.112) correlations are negligible and statistically non-significant. Gender equality has no detectable relationship with how women are represented within prison populations relative to men.

This divergence is itself the central finding of the analysis. When societies become more gender-equal, the absolute rate at which women are imprisoned tends to fall — but this reduction moves in parallel with a comparable shift in male incarceration, leaving the internal gender composition of prisons essentially unchanged. The driver appears to be a systemic effect on the criminal justice system as a whole, not a gender-specific one.

What Theories Tell Us — After the Data

These findings speak directly to the long-standing theoretical debate. The emancipation thesis — that greater gender equality would lead to higher female crime rates — finds no support here. The relationship runs in the opposite direction.

This aligns with Chesney-Lind's marginalization theory: what drives women into contact with criminal justice systems is structural disadvantage, not empowerment. Where gender equality genuinely improves, fewer women end up imprisoned.

The finding also resonates with research on Nordic countries, where Estrada et al. (BJC, 2016) showed that the closing gender gap in crime is driven primarily by a decline in male offending rather than a rise in female criminality. Equality, in this reading, does not lift women toward men's crime rates — it brings the overall system toward historically lower ones.

What the data cannot resolve is causality. Countries with high gender equality also differ in welfare state generosity, incarceration philosophy, policing practices, and legal culture. What the data can do — and does — is challenge a persistent assumption: that more equal societies will see women's behaviour, including criminal behaviour, converge with men's. The European evidence, across a full decade and 27 countries, suggests the opposite.
